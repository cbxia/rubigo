## Move vs Mutability
When defining closures, the keyword `move` forces the closure to **take ownership** of all captured variables, rather than capturing them by reference. The consequence of this is exactly what you described — it **severs the lifetime relationship** between the closure and the environment it was created in.

Without `move`, the closure borrows variables from the surrounding scope, which means the closure's lifetime is constrained to be no longer than those variables' lifetimes. The compiler must track this correlation. With `move`, those variables are *owned* by the closure itself, so there's no lingering reference into the outer scope — the closure becomes a self-contained, independently-lived entity.

This is why `move` closures are essential when:
- **Spawning threads** (`std::thread::spawn`) — the new thread may outlive the current scope, so the closure can't hold references into it
- **Returning closures** from functions — same reasoning; the originating stack frame is gone
- **`async` blocks** — futures are often polled long after the creating scope has ended

The mutability angle is a separate concern entirely — you can have a `move` closure that doesn't mutate anything, or a non-`move` closure that captures `&mut` references. Mutability and ownership transfer are orthogonal.

One small nuance: `move` doesn't *always* transfer ownership in the strictest sense for `Copy` types — an `i32` will be copied into the closure, not "moved" in the destructive sense. But the lifetime-decoupling effect is the same: the closure holds its own copy rather than a reference into the outer scope.

So yes — **"cutting off lifetime correlations"** is a much cleaner mental model than thinking about mutability.
